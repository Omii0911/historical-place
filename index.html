What Is the Taj Mahal?

The Taj Mahal is an immense ivory-white marble mausoleum that rises above the southern bank of the Yamuna River in Agra, India. Commissioned in 1632 by the Mughal emperor Shah Jahan as a resting place for his beloved wife Mumtaz Mahal, it stands as the most celebrated example of Mughal architecture — a tradition that gracefully blends Persian, Ottoman, and Indian artistic sensibilities into a singular, breathtaking whole.

The complex occupies roughly 17 hectares and encompasses a grand gateway, a lush formal garden quartered by shallow reflecting canals, a mosque, a guesthouse, and the central tomb crowned by a massive double-layered dome that soars 73 metres above the ground. Every surface of the mausoleum is adorned with intricate inlay work of semi-precious stones — carnelian, lapis lazuli, jade, turquoise, and coral — forming floral arabesques and Quranic calligraphy of extraordinary delicacy.

Location & History

Agra, situated in the northern Indian state of Uttar Pradesh along the banks of the Yamuna, was the imperial capital of the Mughal Empire during the reigns of Akbar, Jahangir, and Shah Jahan. It is within this city of red sandstone forts and grand gardens that Shah Jahan chose to build his eternal tribute to Mumtaz Mahal, who died in 1631 during the birth of their fourteenth child.

"The sight of this mansion creates sorrowing sighs; and the sun and the moon shed tears from their eyes." — Poet Kalim, court chronicler of Shah Jahan

Construction began in 1632 and drew upon an international workforce of roughly 20,000 artisans, craftsmen, and labourers sourced from across the Mughal Empire, Persia, and Central Asia. The chief architect is believed to have been Ustad Ahmad Lahauri, though a council of architects oversaw different aspects of the project. The main dome was completed around 1648, with the surrounding complex finished by 1653 — a project spanning more than two decades.

Historical Importance

The Taj Mahal is universally regarded as the crowning achievement of Mughal architecture and one of the finest buildings ever constructed by human hands. It was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1983, recognised for its outstanding universal value as a masterpiece of architectural harmony, symmetry, and decorative art. Today, it attracts approximately six to eight million visitors each year, making it one of the world's most visited cultural monuments.

Beyond its architectural significance, the Taj Mahal endures as a profound symbol of love, grief, and imperial devotion. It represents the apex of the Mughal era — a civilisation that fused Timurid, Persian, and indigenous Indian traditions into a distinctive cultural florescence. Shah Jahan himself was later imprisoned by his son Aurangzeb in the nearby Agra Fort, where he spent his final years gazing across the river at the mausoleum he had built for his beloved wife — a poignant coda to a remarkable reign.

Fascinating Facts

01

The marble appears to change colour throughout the day — pinkish at dawn, blinding white at noon, and golden under the full moon.

02

The four minarets surrounding the tomb lean slightly outward so that, in the event of an earthquake, they would fall away from the central dome.

03

An estimated 1,000 elephants were used to transport heavy marble and building materials from Makrana, Rajasthan — over 200 km away.

04

The calligraphy inscribed around the arched entrances is designed with letters that gradually increase in size, creating an optical illusion of uniform height when viewed from below.

05

During World War II, the British government erected a bamboo scaffolding around the dome to disguise it from enemy bombers flying overhead.

06

The garden follows the Persian "Charbagh" plan — divided into four equal quadrants by shallow water channels symbolising the four rivers of Paradise.
